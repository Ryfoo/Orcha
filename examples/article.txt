The Rise of Renewable Energy in 2026

Solar and wind capacity have grown faster than any prior energy transition in
modern history. In 2025 alone, more than 600 gigawatts of new solar were
installed worldwide, with China, the European Union, and the United States
each setting national records.

Battery storage has been the quiet force behind the scenes. Costs per
kilowatt-hour have fallen by roughly 90% since 2015, finally making it
economical to pair intermittent generation with overnight demand. As a
result, a handful of regional grids have run on more than 70% renewables for
weeks at a time without curtailment.

Critics still point to grid stability, mineral supply chains, and permitting
delays as open problems. But the trajectory is no longer in dispute: the
2020s are turning out to be the decade in which clean electricity stopped
being a niche product and became the default.
